Cytogenetics is a branch of genetics that studies the structure of chromosomes to diagnose and monitor acquired and inherited abnormalities. (1) The field of cytogenetics expands from prenatal to oncology analysis using various type of samples such as amniotic fluid, peripheral blood, and bone marrow. (1) The main responsibility of a cytogenetic technologist is to karyotype human chromosome preparations using different computer imaging systems to help doctors diagnose genetic disorders (inherited abnormalities) and different types of cancer (acquired abnormalities) (1). This is an example of a normal human karyotype that does not have any abnormalities in their chromosome Cytogenetic technologists can also be part of making these chromosome preparation in a wet lab. After collecting a sample, the main goal is to try to arrest cells during the metaphase stage of the cell cycle. This is because at this point the chromosomes are aligned in the metaphase plate and will be the most visible under a microscope. One common chemical used for this is called colcomid which works by interfering with the spindle fiber that separates the chromosomes (3). Therefore, the cells will never advance into anaphase and will be stuck in the metaphase stage. These preparations then go through different processes such as hypotonic treatment, G banding staining, and slide dropping so a cytogenetic technologist can analyze them and create a karyotype that shows the results of the analysis. If there is a change or abnormality in certain genes these might be visible in a karyotype. For example, one common abnormality in the cancer type CML (Chronic Myelogenous Leukemia) is a translocation between the chromosomes 9 and 22 (4).
